Matched Pairs

Confidence Interval for the Difference in Means (Matched Pairs)

The confidence interval that we examined in the last handout applies when we are working with two independent samples. Often, however, it makes sense to examine the effect of a treatment on samples that are in some sense related. These samples are called matched pairs. One way a matched pair can form is if each unit that is sampled (a person or object) is studied before and after a treatment is applied. The two sets of data–measurements before and after treatment–will not be independent because the same entity is being measured both times. Another way to form matched pairs is to make groups that are related genetically (e.g. twins), by proximity to one another (e.g., neighbors), by time of measurement (e.g., months of the year), by relationship to one another (e.g., husband/wife), or in some other fashion (e.g., socioeconomic status).

When we have a matched pairs design, the analysis is actually easier than for the two independent samples case. What we do, essentially, is make a new variable that represents the differences between the groups on the measure of interest, and then perform one-sample techniques for $\mu_{d},$ the population mean difference. We need to calculate $\overline{x_{D}},$ the sample mean difference, and $s_{D},$ the standard deviation of the differences. Both of these are calculated using the number of pairs, $n_{d}.$ The formula for the confidence interval is given now.

A $(1-\alpha )100%$ confidence interval for the mean difference between two related groups, denoted $\mu_{d},$ is

$$\overline{x_{D}}\pm t_{\alpha /2,n_{d}}\frac{s_{D}}{\sqrt{n_{d}}}$$

The general four-step procedure for hypothesis testing still applies. What changes are the specifics. Let’s see how.

  1. Specify the hypothesis:

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a) }H_{0} &:&\mu_{d}\leq d\text{ vs. }H_{1}:\mu_{d}>d \ \text{(b) }H_{0} &:&\mu_{d}\geq d\text{ vs. }H_{1}:\mu_{d}<d\text{ } \ \text{(c) }H_{0} &:&\mu_{d}=d\text{ vs. }H_{1}:\mu_{d}\neq d\end{aligned}$$

  2. Calculate the test statistic

    We will assume from now on that we never know the true standard deviation of the population, so our statistic will be a $t.$

    $$t_{0}=\frac{\overline{x_{d}}-d}{\frac{s_{d}}{\sqrt{n_{d}}}}$$

  3. Compute the rejection region

    The the rejection region (i.e., the "critical values") for a test with the $% \alpha$ level of significance would be computed as follows:

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a)}{\text{ }t_{n_{d}-1} &:&t_{n_{d}-1}>\text{ }t_{\alpha ,_{n_{d}-1}}} \ \text{(b) }{t_{n_{d}-1} &:&t_{n_{d}-1}<-t_{\alpha ,n_{d}-1}} \ \text{(c) }{t_{n_{d}-1} &:&|t_{n_{d}-1}|>\text{ }t_{\alpha /2,n_{d}-1}}\end{aligned}$$

    where $t_{n_{d}-1}$is a Student’s $t$ random variable with $n_{d}-1$ degrees of freedom.

  4. Make a conclusion

If the $p$-value $\leq \alpha ,$ we reject $H_{0}.$ If the $p$-value $% >\alpha ,$ we fail to reject $H_{0}.$ Equivalently, if $t_{0}$ lies within the rejection region, we reject $H_{0}$. If $t_{0}$ lies outside the rejection region, we fail to reject $H_{0}.$

Perform a hypothesis test at the $\alpha =0.05$ level for all of the examples above.
