Test for Diff in Means, Proportions

A Hypothesis Test for Two Population/Process Means (Independent Samples)

Here is the procedure for testing the difference between two population/process means from independent samples. You can see that it follows the general pattern that we have seen.

  1. Specify the hypotheses.

    a) $H_{0}:\mu_{1}-\mu_{2}\leq d\text{ vs. }H_{1}:\mu_{1}-\mu_{2}>d$
    b) $H_{0}:\mu_{1}-\mu_{2}\geq d\text{ vs. }H_{1}:\mu_{1}-\mu_{2}<d$ c) $H_{0}:\mu_{1}-\mu_{2}=d\text{ vs. }H_{1}:\mu_{1}-\mu_{2}\neq d$

    where $-\infty <d$ $<\infty$ is some specified difference (usually $0$, but it could be anything$)$.

  2. Calculate the test statistic.

    Case 1: $\sigma _{1}=\sigma _{2}$ (the only case we willconsider in this class)

    $$t_{0}=\frac{\overline{x}_{1}-\overline{x}_{2}-d}{s_{p}\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_{1}}+% \frac{1}{n_{2}}}}$$

    where $s_{p}=\sqrt{\frac{(n_{1}-1)s_{1}^{2}+(n_{2}-1)s_{2}^{2}}{n_{1}+n_{2}-2% }}$ is the pooled standard deviation estimate.

    Case 2: $\sigma _{1}\neq \sigma _{2}$

    $$t_{0}=\frac{\overline{x}_{1}-\overline{x}_{2}-d}{\sqrt{\frac{s_{1}^{2}}{n_{1}% }+\frac{s_{2}^{2}}{n_{2}}}}$$

  3. Compute the p-value

    Case 1: $\sigma _{1}=\sigma _{2}$

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a) }p &=&P(t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}>t_{0}) \text{(b) }p &=&P(t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}<t_{0}) \text{(c) }p &=&2\min [P(t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}>t_{0}),P(t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}<t_{0})]\end{aligned}$$

    Case 2: $\sigma _{1}\neq \sigma _{2}$

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a) }p &=&P(t_{\nu }>t_{0}) \text{(b) }p &=&P(t_{\nu }<t_{0}) \text{(c) }p &=&2\min [P(t_{\nu }>t_{0}),P(t_{\nu }<t_{0})]\end{aligned}$$

    where $\nu =\frac{\left( \frac{s_{1}^{2}}{n_{1}}+\frac{s_{2}^{2}}{n_{2}}% \right) ^{2}}{\frac{\left( \frac{s_{1}^{2}}{n_{1}}\right) ^{2}}{n_{1}-1}+% \frac{\left( \frac{s_{2}^{2}}{n_{2}}\right) ^{2}}{n_{2}-1}}$.

    Alternatively, we can define the rejection region as follows:

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a)}{\text{ }t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2} &:&t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}>\text{ }t_{\alpha ,t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}}} \text{(b) }{t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2} &:&t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}<-t_{\alpha ,t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}}} \text{(c) }{t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2} &:&|t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}|>\text{ }t_{\alpha /2,t_{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}}}\end{aligned}$$

  4. Make a conclusion

    If the $p$-value $\leq \alpha ,$ we reject $H_{0}$. If the $p$-value $% >\alpha ,$ we fail to reject $H_{0}$. Equivalently, if $t_{0}$ lies within the rejection region, we reject $H_{0}$. If $t_{0}$ lies outside the rejection region, we fail to reject $H_{0}$.

    Here are some examples:

In a recent survey of $29$ accounting majors and $38$ general business majors, researchers asked the students to report the amount of time spent studying per week. The sample mean hours for accounting majors was $13.5$ and the sample mean hours for general business majors was $14$. The sample standard deviations are $10$ and $9.8,$ respectively. Determine at the $% \alpha =0.05$ level if there is a difference in the population mean amount of time spent studying per week between accounting and general business majors. Assume the population standard deviations are equal.

Confidence Interval for Difference in Proportion

We might be interested in the difference in the proportions of two groups who meet a certain criterion. For example, we might want to determine what type of website banner ad (one that flashes or one that is steady) leads to a higher proportion of people clicking on it. We might want to determine which of two packaging designs leads to a higher proportion of people who indicate they would buy the product. An excellent example of inference for proportions comes from prescription drug testing. Researchers might be interested to see if a new cancer drug has a reduced chance of causing nausea in patients compared to an existing drug. Opinion polls on issues such as marijuana legalization can be analyzed using techniques for differences in proportions.

The general notation for inference about two population/process proportions is similar to that of two population/process means. The big difference is that the data for both groups can only take on the values of $0$ or $1,$ with $1$ indicating a "success" (either a click on an ad, an indication to purchase, or a person indicating that they did not experience nausea, for example).

Metric Group 1 Group 2
Sample of size $n_{1}:$ $X_{11},X_{12},\ldots ,X_{1n_{1}}$ , all either $0$ or $1$ $n_{2}:$ $X_{21},X_{22},\ldots ,X_{2n_{2}},$ all either $0$ or $1$
Mean: $p_{1}$ $p_{2}$
Sample Proportion $\widehat{p_{1}}=\frac{\sum_{j}x_{1j}}{n_{1}}$ (i.e., number of successes in Group 1) $\widehat{p_{2}}=\frac{\sum_{j}x_{2j}}{n_{2}}$ (i.e., number of successes in Group 2)
Estimated Standard Deviation $\sqrt{\frac{\widehat{p_{1}}(1-\widehat{p_{1}{n_1}}$ $\sqrt{\frac{\widehat{p_{2}}(1-\widehat{p_{2}}{n_{2}}}$

We can now introduce the confidence interval for the difference in two proportions.

A $100(1-\alpha )%$ confidence interval for the difference in  two population proportions $p_{1}-p_{2}$, if the two sample sizes $n_{1}$ and $% n_{2}$ are sufficiently large, is given by

$$\widehat{p_{1}}-\widehat{p_{2}}\pm z_{\alpha /2}\sqrt{\frac{\widehat{p_{1}}% (1-\widehat{p_{1}})}{n_{1}}+\frac{\widehat{p_{2}}(1-\widehat{p_{2}})}{n_{2}}}$$

Just as with means, we might be interested in testing whether two population proportions are equal. We will discuss this in the next section.

Hypothesis Test for Difference in Proportions

The general four-step procedure still applies. What changes are the specifics. Let’s see how.

  1. Specify the hypothesis:

    Now instead of $\mu {1}-\mu {2},$ we work with the population proportions, $p{1}-p{2}:$

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a) }H_{0} &:&p_{1}-p_{2}\leq 0\text{ vs. }H_{1}:p_{1}-p_{2}>0\text{ } \text{(b) }H_{0} &:&p_{1}-p_{2}\geq 0\text{ vs. }H_{1}:p_{1}-p_{2}<0 \text{(c) }H_{0} &:&p_{1}-p_{2}=0\text{ vs. }H_{1}:p_{1}-p_{2}\neq 0\end{aligned}$$

    Note that now we explicitly say that $d=0$. It doesn’t have to be, but if we consider nonzero differences, we must change the test statistic. The additional burden of describing two cases ($d=0$ versus $d$ being nonzero) is not warranted, since testing the zero difference is far more common.

  2. Calculate the test statistic

    The test statistic will always be a $z$ statistic since we assume $n$ is large:

    $$z_{0}=\frac{\widehat{p_{1}}-\widehat{p_{2}}}{\sqrt{\widehat{p}% _{p}(1-p_{p})\left( \frac{1}{n_{1}}+\frac{1}{n_{2}}\right) }}$$

    where $\widehat{p}{p}$ is the "pooled" proportion of successes, i.e., $% \widehat{p}{p}=\frac{\sum_{j}x_{1j}+\sum_{j}x_{2j}}{n_{1}+n_{2}}=\frac{% \text{Total Number of “Successes”}}{\text{Total Sample Size}}$

  3. Compute the p-value or rejection region

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a) }pval &=&P(Z>z_{0}) \text{(b) }pval &=&P(Z<z_{0}) \text{(c) }pval &=&2\min [P(Z>z_{0}),P(Z<z_{0})]\end{aligned}$$

    The the rejection region (i.e., the "critical values") for a test with the $% \alpha$ level of significance would be computed as follows:

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a)}{\text{ }z &:&z>\text{ }z_{\alpha }} \text{(b) }{z &:&z<-z_{\alpha }} \text{(c) }{z &:&|z|>\text{ }z_{\alpha /2}}\end{aligned}$$

  4. Make a conclusion

If the $p$-value $\leq \alpha ,$ we reject $H_{0}$. If the $p$-value $% >\alpha ,$ we fail to reject $H_{0}$. Equivalently, if $z_{0}$ lies within the rejection region, we reject $H_{0}$. If $z_{0}$ lies outside the rejection region, we fail to reject $H_{0}$.
