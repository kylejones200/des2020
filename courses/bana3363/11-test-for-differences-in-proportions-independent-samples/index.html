Hypothesis Test for a Difference in Proportion (Independent Samples)

Hypothesis Test for a Difference in Proportion (Independent Samples)

Just as with means, we might be interested in testing whether two population proportions are equal. The general four-step procedure still applies. What changes are the specifics. Let’s see how.

  1. Specify the hypothesis:

    Now instead of $\mu {1}-\mu {2},$ we work with the population proportions, $p{1}-p{2}:$

    $$\begin{aligned} \text{(a) }H_{0} &:&p_{1}-p_{2}\leq 0\text{ vs. }H_{1}:p_{1}-p_{2}>0\text{ } \ \text{(b) }H_{0} &:&p_{1}-p_{2}\geq 0\text{ vs. }H_{1}:p_{1}-p_{2}<0 \ \text{(c) }H_{0} &:&p_{1}-p_{2}=0\text{ vs. }H_{1}:p_{1}-p_{2}\neq 0\end{aligned}$$

    Note that now we explicitly say that $d=0$. It doesn’t have to be, but if we consider nonzero differences, we must change the test statistic. The additional burden of describing two cases ($d=0$ versus $d$ being nonzero) is not warranted for our purposes, since testing the zero difference is far more common.

  2. Calculate the test statistic

    The test statistic will always be a $z$ statistic since we assume $n$ is large:

    $$z=\frac{\widehat{p_{1}}-\widehat{p_{2}}}{\sqrt{\widehat{p}% _{p}(1-p_{p})\left( \frac{1}{n_{1}}+\frac{1}{n_{2}}\right) }}$$

    where $\widehat{p}{p}$ is the “pooled” proportion of successes, i.e., $% \widehat{p}{p}=\frac{\sum_{j}x_{1j}+\sum_{j}x_{2j}}{n_{1}+n_{2}}=\frac{% \text{Total Number of “Successes”}}{\text{Total Sample Size}}$

  3. Determine the rejection region or compute the $p$-value.

The the rejection region for a test with the $\alpha$ level of significance would be determined using the standard normal distribution using the following critical values. Note that these are the same critical values that we used for the test of a single proportion.

$$\begin{aligned} \text{(a)}{\text{ }z &:&z>\text{ }z_{\alpha }} \ \text{(b) }{z &:&z<-z_{\alpha }} \ \text{(c) }{z &:&|z|>\text{ }z_{\alpha /2}}\end{aligned}$$

Alternatively, we can compute the $p$-value as follows:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{(a) }pval &=&P(Z>z) \ \text{(b) }pval &=&P(Z<z) \ \text{(c) }pval &=&2\min [P(Z>z),P(Z<z)]\end{aligned}$$

  1. Make a conclusion

If the $p$-value $\leq \alpha ,$ we reject $H_{0}.$ If the $p$-value $% >\alpha ,$ we fail to reject $H_{0}.$ Equivalently, if $z$ lies within the rejection region, we reject $H_{0}$. If $z$ lies outside the rejection region, we fail to reject $H_{0}.$

A website designer wants to examine the effectiveness of two different layouts (A and B) for a small retail client in terms of the proportion of individuals who find and click on a special coupon within the first five minutes of browsing the retailer’s site. Presumably, the more people who find and click the coupon, the more effective the layout is. For two weeks, whenever a customer accessed the retailer’s site, one of the two layouts (A or B) was randomly chosen to load on the customer’s screen. Customers were tracked by their IP addresses, numbers that uniquely identify devices over the internet. Of the customers who browsed the site for at least five minutes, the proportion who clicked the coupon using each layout was recorded. The data are below. At the  $\alpha =0.01$ level of significance, can the website designer conclude that the layouts differ in effectiveness?

Example: Astrology

Astrology is a system of belief that, broadly speaking, claims that astronomical events somehow influence human behavior. One aspect of astrology is belief that the zodiac sign that one is born under (e.g., Pisces, Libra, Leo, Scorpio, etc.) somehow influences a person’s behavior. Is belief in astrology related significantly to education level? The $2012$ General Social Survey asked adults $18$ and over whether they believed at all in the zodiac.
